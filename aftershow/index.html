The Problem

Concert Archives has deep data. Zero soul.

Die-hard concert fans have been to 50, 100, 200+ shows. That history lives in their heads, in old ticket stubs in a shoebox, in Instagram posts they can't find anymore. The one app that tries to catalogue this — Concert Archives — is built like a database, not a diary. It gatekeeps: you need to verify you were there. It treats attendance like something that needs to be proven.

Nobody builds for the fan who saw Radiohead in 2003 and remembers every song. Nobody treats the archive as identity rather than data. That's the gap.

→
Gap 1: No lifetime diary

Concert Archives requires event data to exist in their system. If the show isn't in the database, you can't log it. That eliminates every obscure gig, every small venue, every show from 15 years ago.

→
Gap 2: Archive as identity

Your concert history says something about who you are. Existing apps treat it as a list. Aftershow treats it as a record — something worth presenting, sharing, and being proud of.

→
Gap 3: Private-first social

Concert Archives is a database. Social features feel bolted on. Aftershow designs the archive as the diary first — shareable second. The Letterboxd model.

The Core Differentiator

From Memory. No gatekeeping.

The "from memory" log is what no other app has. You can add any show you've ever attended — no ticket, no check-in, no proof required. Just artist, venue, date, setlist as best you remember, and a rating. Shows logged from memory get a subtle tag — not a warning, not a badge of shame. It's just context.

This one decision unlocks the lifetime diary use case. A 45-year-old who's been to 300 shows since 1998 can build their full archive. A casual fan who went to one Taylor Swift show last year can log it. No gatekeeping, ever.

1
Utility over decoration — the setlist is the hero
2
The show is the product — every flow leads to the "+" button
3
No gatekeeping — "from memory" is a feature, not a warning

5
Respect the culture — designed for people who've stood in a pit at 11pm on a Tuesday
Architecture Decisions

My Shows is Tab 1. Not the feed.

Every competing app puts the social feed first. Aftershow puts your personal archive first. This is the biggest structural departure and it was the first decision made after the competitive analysis.

→
My Shows (Archive) = Tab 1

The archive is the product. The feed is the reward. Flipping this establishes Aftershow as a personal diary first — not another social app with a logging feature.

→
Onboarding starts with your history — not your profile

When you first open Aftershow, you don't pick a username and upload a photo. You log a show. That's it. The archive must be seeded before the social layer has any value — so we lead with the action that creates that value.

→
1-show minimum, not 3

Original thinking was a 3-show threshold for onboarding. Cut to 1. A 3-show gate alienates users who've only been to one or two concerts — exactly the casual fans who could grow into power users. Lower the bar, let people in.

→
Log a Show is the center CTA

Tab 3 is a prominent "+" button, not a screen. Every flow in the app leads back to this action. It's the Shazam button of Aftershow — the single most important interaction.

→
Design both themes simultaneously

Dark and light modes were designed in parallel from day one, not as an afterthought. Gold (#D4A853) is the single consistent accent across both — the connective thread that makes the product feel cohesive regardless of mode.

Design System

Typography does the heavy lifting

Four typefaces, all with clear jobs. The pairing of editorial serif (Playfair Display) with mono (DM Mono) gives Aftershow a premium, almost printed-program feel that no other concert app has.

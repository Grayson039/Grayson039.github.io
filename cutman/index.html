The Problem

Five Apps to Follow One Sport

I follow all of it — UFC, boxing, BKFC, BJJ, Kombat Karate, PFL, ONE Championship. And every single time I want to know when the next event is, where to watch, who's on the card, what the odds are, what's in the news, or who a fighter's corner coach is — I'm bouncing between five different apps and websites. None of them cover all the promotions. None surface fighter news and corner bios alongside the fight card.

There is no hub for combat sports fans that spans all disciplines. There should be.

Pain Point

No cross-promotion coverage

UFC.com covers UFC only. ESPN buries fight cards in general sports noise. Boxing, BKFC, BJJ, and Kombat Karate fans have no home at all.

Pain Point

Fighter context is scattered

Fighter news, bios, stats, and corner information live across Wikipedia, Tapology, and promotion sites — none of it connected or presented for a fan.

The Insight

Fans Don't Need More Sources

The insight that drove every design decision: fans don't need another source. They need one source that thinks like a fan. That means cross-promotion aggregation across MMA, boxing, BKFC, BJJ, and Kombat Karate — surfacing fight cards, fighter news, bios, stats, and corner information all in one place, with depth a tap away rather than buried across five different sites.

The design challenge isn't the information — it's the hierarchy. Combat sports fans want everything, but density is the enemy of usefulness. Every screen had to earn its information. A casual fan should be able to open it and immediately know what's happening this week. A die-hard should be able to drill into a fighter's record, corner staff, and gym connections without leaving the app.

Screen 01 · Home

The Next Fight, Above the Fold

The home screen has one job: tell you what's coming up and what you need to know before you go deeper. The hero card always shows the next major event — promotion, fighters, date, venue, network, and PPV cost in one glance. No tapping around to find out if you need to pay.

→
Hero card takes 40% of the screen

The next event is the most time-sensitive piece of information. It gets the most real estate. Everything else lives below it.

→
Promotion filter chips, not a dropdown

Boxing fans shouldn't have to scroll through UFC cards. Filter chips let you narrow by promotion in one tap and stay narrow until you want to broaden.

→
Color-coded promo badges

UFC in red, Boxing in purple, PFL in green, ONE in gold. A fan scanning a list of events can identify their promotion instantly without reading the label.

→
Removed the hexagon texture

Early versions had a subtle red hexagon pattern on the screen background. Design review feedback was that it cluttered the home screen. Cut it. The fighter profile hero still uses it implicitly through the photo treatment.

Screen 02 · Fight Card

Every Fight, Ranked by Importance

The fight card screen surfaces the full event card in a scannable list. Main card at top, prelims collapsible below. Each fight row shows fighters, rankings, and live betting odds inline — because fans check odds constantly and burying them would make this no better than UFC.com.

→
Odds inline in the fight row

Odds aren't supplementary — they're primary for fight fans. They sit in the VS column between fighters, not behind a tap.

→
Title fights get gold treatment

Championship bouts get a gold background tint and the 🏆 weight class label. Glanceable hierarchy — you know what's important without reading everything.

→
Prelims are collapsible

Casual fans care about the main card. Die-hards care about everything. Prelims collapse by default — one tap to open. Nobody gets what they don't want.

Screen 03 · Fighter Profile

Record, Corner, Bio — In That Order

Fighter profiles are tabbed: Record, Corner, Bio. The tab order is intentional. Fans want the record first. The Corner tab is Cutman's most unique feature — it shows who else trains at the same gym, surfacing the gym-fighter connection graph that hardcore fans know and casual fans have never seen presented this way.

→
Corner tab: the differentiator

No other combat sports app surfaces the training camp connection. Knowing Jon Jones and Max Holloway both come from Jackson-Wink is fight-fan context that makes you sound like an expert. That's the casual-to-expert bridge.

→
Physical stats in one bar

Age, height, weight, reach — four numbers that matter before every fight. They live in a single row inside the hero card, not buried in the Bio tab.

→
Win method breakdown on the hero

KO/TKO, SUB, DEC — color coded in red, gold, green. A fighter's style lives in those three numbers. They're on the hero card, not in a stats tab.

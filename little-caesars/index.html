What Real Users Were Saying

Before touching a single screen, I reviewed recent App Store ratings and 1–3 star reviews. Three friction patterns showed up again and again — and every design decision in this redesign maps directly back to them.

Finding 01

The menu was impossible to browse

Users couldn’t see a single menu item without committing to a location, order type, and pickup time first. The ordering flow was built for the restaurant — not the customer.

“This app makes ordering harder than necessary. The menu is an insanely long scroll list and the categories are basically hidden since you can’t jump to them.”

★★☆☆☆ — Matango88, Jan 2026

“Prices and deals are only available when ordered through the app, but it’s very hard to see where they are.”

★☆☆☆☆ — Kathy tbh, May 2026
Finding 02

Customization was broken or paywalled

Users wanting basic modifications — no salt, party cut, light sauce — had no path forward except upgrading to a pricier “Create Your Own” option. The app was training customers to call in instead of ordering digitally.

“How do you have a pizza app that doesn’t allow you to customize your toppings? If you want no onions or light sauce, you can’t do it.”

★☆☆☆☆ — LJD062, May 2026

“I love the pretzel crust but can’t handle all the salt. My only options are to order in person or call in — both slow down the staff.”

★★★★☆ — billconiglio, Mar 2026
Finding 03

Order trust had completely broken down

The “order ready” notification was meaningless. Users arrived to find food wasn’t ready, loyalty points disappeared without explanation, and promos failed at checkout.

“The app said it would be ready at 6:16. Got there at 6:18 — not ready. Was asked to drive around the building and come back 40 minutes later.”

★☆☆☆☆ — bravo52, May 2026

“I had accumulated hundreds of Little Caesar points. My account got deleted and now I have to start back from zero.”

★☆☆☆☆ — durnit54, May 2026

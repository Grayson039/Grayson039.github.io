The Problem

$100 for Feedback You Shouldn't Have to Pay For

Professional script coverage costs $30–$100 per read. Writers submit blind, get rejected without feedback, and have no way to improve. The only writers who can afford real feedback are the ones who already have money and connections.

That's not a feedback gap. That's a gatekeeping problem.

The entertainment industry has always filtered talent by access — access to film school, to agents, to the right rooms. The writers who most need feedback are exactly the ones least likely to get it.

Design Philosophy

Writers Are Emotionally Vulnerable. The Design Knows That.

Script coverage is high-stakes feedback on months or years of work. Every interaction pattern was built with that emotional context in mind.

Win First, Always

Every score is preceded by a specific, data-driven win — not generic encouragement. Real, page-referenced praise before any critique.

Reframed Status Language

"Building Momentum" replaces Pass. "Submission Ready" replaces Recommend. The same information, without the sting of industry jargon.

Fix It → Inline

Every flagged note has a Fix It button — opens an inline editor for just that scene, re-analyzes instantly, logs the revision automatically.

Fix It → Re-analyzes in <2s

Craft, Not Taste

AI grades structure, conflict, dialogue, pacing, visual storytelling. Never subjective taste. "This doesn't interest me" isn't feedback — it's gatekeeping.

The Interesting Detail

Virality Baked Into the Loading Screen

Every time a writer uploads a script, they get a randomly assigned loading experience. Writers start uploading scripts just to see which one they get.

Star Wars Crawl

The script title and logline scroll as an opening crawl. Cinematic. Makes writers feel like their story is already on screen.

"A long time ago, in a galaxy far, far away..."

Film Countdown Leader

The classic 8-7-6-5-4-3-2-1 countdown with film grain. Feels like you're about to watch your own movie.

"Picture lock. Analyzing..."

Clapperboard Animation

An animated clapperboard slates the script with title, draft number, and the "director." Ends on a take.

"Scene One. Take it from the top."
The Design Insight

"Each loading experience ends with a film-specific phrase instead of generic UI copy. Nobody expects a coverage tool to open with 'A long time ago in a galaxy far, far away...' That's the whole point."

The Bigger Mission

The Film School That Never Gatekept Anyone

I couldn't afford film school. Millions of writers can't. SceneOne starts as a coverage tool and grows into an education layer — triggered by your specific script's weaknesses, not a $60,000 tuition bill.

Bad dialogue? SceneOne links you to the exact lesson. Act Two falling apart? The module triggers automatically. Education tied to your work, not a semester calendar.
